# Answer with Books editorial style

## The standard

An Answer with Books page is a finished piece of understanding, not a preview of a book and not an outline for something the reader must read later. A strong page leaves the reader able to explain the central insight, see why it is true, and use it in a real decision.

The governing test is: **what important and non-obvious thing does this page make clear?** Paul Graham's *The Best Essay* is a useful reference because it treats writing as a process of discovery. Begin with a real question, commit to an answer, test the answer for correctness and completeness, and cut branches that do not improve the central idea. Borrow that rigor, not his mannerisms.

## Structure

Write prose first. Use paragraphs to develop an argument in sequence: question, claim, mechanism, evidence or source example, implication. A section should move the reader's understanding forward; it should not merely name a topic.

Use lists only when the material is inherently list-shaped: a procedure, checklist, set of questions, or compact comparison. A page should never read like expanded presentation slides. As a default, paragraphs should substantially outnumber list items.

Headings should state the idea or tension of the section. Prefer “Compliments hide the absence of commitment” to “Core lesson 3.” Do not force every digest into identical headings.

Simple line illustrations are encouraged when they make a causal chain, hierarchy, tradeoff, or sequence easier to understand. Keep them monochrome, labeled, accessible, and small enough to grasp in seconds. Do not add decorative diagrams.

## Book digests

A book digest should be self-contained in roughly 1,200–1,800 words. It should:

- state the book's central claim early;
- explain three to five consequential ideas in connected prose;
- preserve the mechanism that makes each idea true;
- use examples only when they come from the book or another identified source;
- distinguish the author's claim from the editor's interpretation;
- integrate important qualifications where they affect the claim;
- end when the central argument and its practical consequence are complete.

Do not invent a “worked example” to make the digest feel practical. If the book contains a useful example, attribute it directly and explain what it proves. If it does not, omit the example.

Do not include generic “When this lens breaks,” “Best paired with,” or “Related books” template sections. A limitation belongs beside the claim it limits. Catalog discovery and relationships belong in the surrounding product interface, not as an obligatory ending to the digest.

## Answers

An answer begins with the reader's problem, but it must deliver the insight rather than tease the source books. Explain the mechanism, the evidence that matters, and the decision consequence. Cite book pages where their ideas enter the reasoning.

The answer may end with a next move or decision rule when one follows from the argument. It should not bolt on a checklist merely to appear actionable. Any uncertainty or boundary should appear where it changes the recommendation instead of in a mandatory disclaimer section.

Answer briefs should normally run 900–1,400 words, remain paragraph-led, and use four to six idea-led sections. Follow the repeatable research, drafting, and scoring process in [the answer editorial workflow](./ANSWER_EDITORIAL_WORKFLOW.md).

## Tone

Be direct, curious, exact, and calm. Prefer concrete nouns and verbs. Explain jargon when it earns its place. Use short sentences for the claim and longer paragraphs for the reasoning.

Avoid hype, book-report language, motivational filler, and preview language such as “this book will teach you,” “read on to discover,” or “the author explores.” Say what the idea is. Do not praise a book instead of explaining it.

Use quotations sparingly and only when the wording itself carries insight. Verify the wording and keep quoted source material short. Paraphrase the argument in original language.

## Editorial check

Before publishing, ask:

1. Can the reader state the central insight without opening the book?
2. Does each section add a claim, mechanism, evidence, or implication?
3. Are examples source-grounded and attributed?
4. Would removing a list make the argument stronger as prose?
5. Is every qualification placed beside the claim it changes?
6. Did we cut preview copy, generic template sections, related-book appendices, and ornamental advice?
7. Does a diagram clarify something prose alone would make harder to see?
