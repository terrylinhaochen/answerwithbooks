# Source brief: *Noise: A Flaw in Human Judgment* — Daniel Kahneman, Olivier Sibony, Cass R. Sunstein

## Central argument

The book’s central thesis is that human judgment is damaged not only by **bias**—shared, directional error—but also by **noise**, the unwanted variability of judgments that should be similar or identical. The authors’ slogan-level claim, stated on the official book site, is that wherever there is human judgment, there is likely more noise than people expect; the publisher frames the book as explaining why people make bad judgments and how to improve them by controlling both noise and cognitive bias. ([readnoise.com](https://readnoise.com/))

The tension the book resolves is this: modern organizations often know how to talk about bias, because bias has a narrative and a cause, but they often fail to see inconsistency because it is statistical and only visible across cases. Kahneman tells *Behavioral Scientist* that bias is average error while noise is variability of error; he also emphasizes that noise cannot usually be diagnosed in a single judgment but becomes visible in a pattern of judgments. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/)) The book therefore shifts the editor’s frame from “Which bias caused this bad decision?” to “How variable is this decision system, and is that variability acceptable?”

## Mechanisms and frameworks

**Bias versus noise.** Bias is a directional tendency: a forecasting team may be consistently optimistic, or judges may be systematically severe. Noise is scatter: equally authorized judges, physicians, interviewers, underwriters, or evaluators produce different answers when the organization expects them to be broadly interchangeable. McKinsey’s interview with Kahneman and Sibony gives the clean distinction: bias is average error; noise is variability of error. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise)) In contexts with an objectively correct answer, the book uses the error equation: overall error, or mean squared error, equals bias squared plus noise squared. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/)) For evaluative judgments, the same intuition holds even when “truth” is harder to define: unjustified spread in outcomes is itself a quality problem.

**System noise.** The book’s main target is **system noise**: unwanted variability within a system that is supposed to speak with one voice. Examples include judges in the same court, doctors facing similar diagnostic evidence, or insurance underwriters pricing the same risk. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise)) This is not ordinary disagreement in tastes, values, art, politics, or innovation; it is disagreement where the institution itself expects bounded consistency.

**Level noise.** Level noise is variability in the average level of judgments across decision makers. One judge is generally harsher, one interviewer generally more forgiving, one performance rater generally generous. Sibony uses performance reviews to illustrate this: some raters are, on average, more generous than others. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise)) Level noise is often confused with bias because it looks stable, but the key is that the organization has many such individual “levels,” producing inconsistency across the system.

**Pattern noise.** Pattern noise is variability in how judges respond to particular cases, beyond their general severity or leniency. Two judges might be equally severe on average but disagree sharply about which defendants, applicants, claims, or candidates deserve tougher treatment. The authors later clarified in a 2022 exchange that their system-noise framework breaks differences between judges into level noise and pattern noise. ([journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/26339137221078593)) For an editor: level noise is “who is generally high or low?”; pattern noise is “which cases does each person treat as high or low?”

**Occasion noise.** Occasion noise is within-person instability: the same judge, doctor, inspector, or interviewer might decide differently depending on timing, mood, sequence, fatigue, weather, or other irrelevant conditions. The official book site uses morning versus afternoon and Monday versus Wednesday as examples of the same professional making different judgments. ([readnoise.com](https://readnoise.com/)) The 2022 author exchange defines occasion noise as within-person variability and says it contributes to system noise, though the authors describe it as less central than differences between decision makers. ([journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/26339137221078593))

**Noise audit.** A noise audit measures system noise by giving multiple interchangeable decision makers the same realistic cases and asking for precise judgments, often numerical or scaled. Kahneman says the correct answer is not always needed; the audit can simply measure variability. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/)) The insurance-underwriting example is important: executives expected about 10% variation among underwriters, but the observed variation was roughly 55%, revealing a much noisier system than leadership imagined. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/))

**Decision hygiene.** Decision hygiene is the book’s umbrella term for preventive practices that reduce noise before specific errors are visible. Sibony compares it to hygiene because it is preventive rather than a cure for a named mistake; it can feel thankless because users do not know exactly which error they prevented. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise)) Core practices include breaking judgments into subjudgments, delaying holistic intuition, preserving independence before discussion, using relative rather than vague absolute scales where appropriate, selecting more competent judges, and sometimes replacing human judgment with rules, checklists, or algorithms while auditing those tools for bias. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise))

**Independent judgments.** The book treats independence as a causal mechanism: if people discuss too early, social influence, anchoring, authority, and shared stories make errors correlated. Sibony recommends getting people to make judgments independently and only then aggregating or discussing them. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise)) Kahneman similarly says that decision hygiene asks people to break up a problem, make fact-based assessments, keep assessments independent, and delay intuition rather than eliminate it. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/))

**Mediating Assessments Protocol.** MAP is the book’s structured method for complex decisions. It originated in work by Kahneman, Dan Lovallo, and Sibony and was incorporated into *Noise* as a general-purpose decision-hygiene tool. The MIT Sloan Management Review release describes MAP as defining key assessments in advance, using fact-based independent assessments, and postponing the final evaluation until the assessment profile is complete. ([prnewswire.com](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/in-a-significant-new-paper-published-in-mit-sloan-management-review-nobel-laureate-daniel-kahneman-and-strategy-professors-dan-lovallo-and-olivier-sibony-introduce-a-new-approach-to-making-smarter-strategic-decisions-300805463.html)) Sydney Business Insights summarizes the same logic as delaying intuition: define assessment criteria, use independently made fact-based assessments, then make the final evaluation. ([sbi.sydney.edu.au](https://sbi.sydney.edu.au/making-better-decisions-every-time/)) The public digest should explain MAP as a way to stop teams from deciding too early and then retrofitting evidence to a favored story.

## Book-grounded examples

- **Judicial sentencing.** The publisher, official site, McKinsey interview, and *Behavioral Scientist* interview all use judges giving different sentences for similar or identical crimes. This demonstrates system noise and the justice problem: citizens face a lottery when the system expects like cases to be treated alike. ([hachettebookgroup.com](https://www.hachettebookgroup.com/titles/daniel-kahneman/noise/9780316451406/))  
- **Doctors and diagnoses.** The official book site and publisher describe two doctors in the same city giving different diagnoses to identical patients. This demonstrates that noise is not just corporate inconvenience; it can affect health outcomes. ([readnoise.com](https://readnoise.com/))  
- **Insurance underwriters.** Kahneman’s interview describes underwriters judging exactly the same thing and producing far more variability than executives expected. This is the cleanest verified example for a noise audit. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/))  
- **Performance ratings.** Sibony says performance reviews contain level noise and occasion noise, with much of the rating unrelated to actual performance. This example is useful for explaining how organizational assessments can become noisy even when forms and scales appear standardized. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise))  
- **Fingerprint and forensic judgments.** Sibony’s Oxford Review interview says fingerprint identification is a judgment and therefore subject to noise, even in a domain people often imagine to be nearly infallible. ([oxford-review.com](https://oxford-review.com/noise/))  
- **Customer complaints, food inspectors, and job interviews.** The official site uses these as everyday organizational examples: outcomes can depend on who happens to answer the phone, inspect the restaurant, or interview the candidate. ([readnoise.com](https://readnoise.com/))

## Qualifications and common misreadings

Do not present the book as saying that all variability is bad. The authors define noise as **unwanted** variability; diversity of taste, creativity, pluralism, and specialized perspectives can be desirable. Kahneman explicitly says the relevant target is an organization that wants to speak with one voice, while film reviewers or creative teams need not be identical. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/))

Do not present decision hygiene as eliminating judgment or intuition. Kahneman says the goal is to delay intuition, not ban it; people resist systems that make them feel like automatons. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/)) Also avoid claiming that noise can be eliminated. Kahneman says noise, like bias, can be reduced but not completely removed unless life is governed entirely by rules and algorithms. ([behavioralscientist.org](https://behavioralscientist.org/a-conversation-with-daniel-kahneman-about-noise/))

Do not imply that algorithms are automatically fair. Sibony says algorithms can remove human inconsistency, but they can also introduce systematic bias if trained or designed poorly. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise)) Also note cost-benefit limits: the authors later clarified that because noise reduction has costs, the benefits may not always justify the intervention. ([journals.sagepub.com](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/26339137221078593))

Finally, treat the book as ambitious but not the last word. Kahneman called the project somewhat premature or “green,” noting that a larger research program would ideally take much longer. ([mckinsey.com](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/strategy-and-corporate-finance/our-insights/sounding-the-alarm-on-system-noise))
